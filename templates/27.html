Information:

Grape anthracnose, also known as bird's eye rot, is a fungal disease that can affect grapes. The disease is caused by the fungus Elsinoe ampelina, and can cause significant damage to grapevines if left untreated.

Symptoms:

  • Small, round, dark spots on leaves - These spots can be up to 1/4 inch in diameter and may have a lighter-colored center.
  • Sunken lesions on fruit - Infected fruit may develop sunken, dark-colored lesions with a characteristic "bird's eye" appearance.
  • Stem cankers - The fungus can cause cankers on grapevine stems, which can eventually girdle and kill the vine.
  • Premature defoliation - Infected leaves may yellow and fall off prematurely, which can weaken the vine and reduce yield.
  • Reduced fruit quality - Infected fruit may have reduced sugar content, which can affect the quality of the wine or juice produced.

Treatment:

  • Remove and destroy infected plant material - Infected leaves, canes, and fruit should be removed and destroyed to prevent the spread of the disease.
  • Practice good sanitation - Keep the vineyard clean and free of debris, which can harbor fungal spores. Prune vines regularly to promote good air circulation and sunlight penetration.
  • Apply fungicides - Fungicides can be applied to control anthracnose, but they should be used as part of an integrated pest management program.
  • All infected plant parts should be burned, buried or sent to a municipal composting site.
  • follow the label instructions carefully and apply the fungicide at the recommended time.
  • Monitor for re-infection - Keep an eye out for signs of re-infection, and take prompt action if the disease reappears.

Fertilizer recommend:

  • Soil testing - Before applying fertilizer, it's important to have your soil tested to determine its nutrient levels and pH. This information can help you select the appropriate fertilizer and application rate.
  • Nitrogen - Grapevines have high nitrogen requirements, especially during the early stages of growth However, excessive nitrogen can promote vegetative growth at the expense of fruit quality.
  • Phosphorus and potassium - Phosphorus and potassium are also important for grapevine growth and fruit quality A soil test can help determine if these nutrients are deficient and in need of supplementation.
  • Micronutrients - Grapevines also require small amounts of micronutrients, such as iron, zinc, and manganese.
  • Timing - Fertilizer should be applied at the appropriate time to ensure maximum uptake by the grapevines.
