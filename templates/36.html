Guava scab is a fungal disease caused by the plant pathogen Mycosphaerella psidii. The disease is most common in areas with high humidity and can affect both commercial and backyard guava trees. The disease primarily affects the leaves and fruit of the guava tree, leading to reduced fruit quality and yield.

Symptoms:

  • The symptoms typically begin as small, raised, circular or oval-shaped spots on the upper surface of the leaf.
  • These spots may be brown, black, or gray in color and may have a velvety texture. As the disease progresses, the spots may grow larger and merge together, forming larger lesions.
  • The affected areas of the leaf may become distorted, with a puckered or wrinkled appearance. Severely infected leaves may turn yellow and drop prematurely from the tree.
  • In severe cases, guava scab can cause defoliation of the tree, which can lead to reduced fruit quality and yield.

Treatment:

  • Cultural control: Cultural practices such as proper orchard sanitation, pruning, and removal of diseased plant material can help to reduce the severity of guava scab. Dead leaves and debris should be removed from the orchard floor and destroyed to prevent the disease from spreading.
  • Fungicides: Fungicides can be used to control guava scab. Copper-based fungicides such as copper sulfate can be effective, but should be used at the appropriate rate and frequency to avoid phytotoxicity. Other fungicides that can be used include azoxystrobin, pyraclostrobin, and trifloxystrobin.
  • Organic control: Organic methods such as the use of neem oil or baking soda sprays can also be effective against guava scab. These methods may not provide complete control of the disease, but can help to reduce the severity of the symptoms.
  • Genetic resistance: Some guava cultivars have been reported to have genetic resistance to guava scab. Planting resistant cultivars can be an effective long-term strategy to manage the disease.

Fertilizer recommend:

  • Fertilizers: Guava trees require a balanced fertilizer regimen, including nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and micronutrients. Nitrogen is important for the growth and development of leaves and branches, while phosphorus is necessary for the formation of flowers and fruit. Potassium is important for overall tree health and disease resistance. Common fertilizers used for guava trees include 10-10-10, 15-15-15, and 20-20-20.

These fungicides are effective against a wide range of fungal diseases, including guava scab. However, overuse of copper-based fungicides can lead to phytotoxicity, which can damage the tree and reduce yield.
  • Azoxystrobin: Azoxystrobin is a fungicide that belongs to the class of strobilurin fungicides. It is effective against a range of fungal diseases, including guava scab. Azoxystrobin can be used as a preventive or curative treatment and is usually applied as a foliar spray.
  • Propiconazole: Propiconazole is a systemic fungicide that can be used to control guava scab. It is absorbed by the plant and translocated to the leaves and fruit. Propiconazole is effective against a range of fungal diseases, including guava scab. It is usually applied as a foliar spray.
