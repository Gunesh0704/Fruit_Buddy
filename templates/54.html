Common leaf spot of strawberry (also known as Mycosphaerella leaf spot, Ramularia leaf spot, strawberry leaf spot, birdâ€™s-eye spot, gray spotness, and white spot) is a common fungal leaf disease that affects both wild and cultivated strawberries throughout the world. Common leaf spot was once the most economically important strawberry disease, but the use of resistant strawberry varieties/cultivars and improvements in methods for growing strawberries have been effective in managing the disease and reducing its impact.

Symptoms:

  • Small, dark spots on the leaves: These spots can vary in size from small dots to larger irregular shapes. They may start off as a purplish color and eventually turn dark brown or black.
  • Yellowing of the leaves: As the infection progresses, the leaves around the spots may turn yellow and die off.
  • Spots on the fruit: In severe cases, the fungus may infect the fruit as well, causing small dark spots to appear.
  • Premature leaf drop: The infected leaves may drop off the plant prematurely, which can weaken the plant and reduce its yield.

Treatment:

  • Remove infected leaves: As soon as you notice leaf spot on your strawberry plants, remove any infected leaves and dispose of them in the trash (not in your compost). This can help prevent the fungus from spreading to other parts of the plant.
  • Improve air circulation: Leaf spot thrives in damp, humid conditions, so improving air circulation around your strawberry plants can help prevent the fungus from spreading. You can do this by pruning back any overgrown or crowded areas around the plants.
  • Water at the right time: Watering your plants in the morning can help prevent leaf spot by allowing the leaves to dry off during the day. Avoid watering in the evening or at night, as this can create a damp environment that is favorable to fungal growth.
  • Apply fungicides: If the leaf spot is severe, you may need to apply a fungicide to prevent further spread. Look for a fungicide that is specifically labeled for use on strawberries, and follow the instructions carefully.
  • Practice good plant hygiene: To prevent future outbreaks of leaf spot, practice good plant hygiene by removing any dead or diseased plant material, keeping the area around your plants clean and free of debris, and rotating your crops to prevent the buildup of fungal spores in the soil.

Fertilizer recommend:

  • Nitrogen: Nitrogen is essential for vegetative growth, and strawberries require a steady supply throughout the growing season. Apply nitrogen fertilizer in early spring before flowering, and again in late summer after fruiting is complete. A balanced fertilizer with equal amounts of nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium (e.g. 10-10-10) can be a good choice.
  • Phosphorus: Phosphorus is important for root development and fruiting. Apply a phosphorus-rich fertilizer (e.g. 0-45-0) at planting time to encourage strong root growth.
  • Potassium: Potassium helps with overall plant health and disease resistance. Apply a potassium-rich fertilizer (e.g. 0-0-50) in the fall after fruiting is complete to help plants prepare for winter.
